# Chapter 13: Green Letters

The first true terminal room was built in `1844`.

Not the first display room. Not the first machine room. The first place where a
person not directly involved in the underlying mechanism could sit before a
screen, press keys, and interact with a distant system in text.

The room itself was disappointingly plain. Fiona approved. Whitewashed walls,
ventilation ducts that refused to be dignified, cable runs arranged with more
honesty than elegance, and along one side a row of cabinets whose glow, when the
machines settled into work, cast a faint greenish wash across brass fittings.

The screens were not modern, of course. Nor cheap. Nor domestic. They belonged
to institutions that could afford delicacy and electricians both. Yet they
worked.

A clerk could call a ledger extract from another office.
A port authority could request a shipping state.
A scientific correspondent in Paris could send a symbolic procedure package to
London and receive a checked result back before the day ended.
A message could be typed, addressed, queued, routed, and answered without once
touching paper until someone chose to print it.

Before the room opened formally, Fiona insisted on two weeks of user trials.
This shocked the committee, which thought opening a technical marvel to ordinary
clerks before dignitaries had admired it amounted to sacrilege.

That, in Fiona's opinion, was why the trials were necessary.

The first users were exactly the people grand projects usually treated as
furniture.

- a shipping clerk with bad handwriting but excellent memory
- a customs officer of suspicious temperament
- a scientific assistant from Paris who mistrusted British engineering on
  patriotic grounds
- two exhausted message operators
- one accountant who believed every machine was secretly designed to insult him

Within three days they had improved the terminal room more than any committee
could have done in six months.

The accountant demanded clearer state prompts. The customs officer found an
addressing ambiguity. The Paris assistant identified a fault in one display
sequence that made two symbols indistinguishable at certain brightness. One of
the operators asked why the keyboard had been laid out according to abstract
beauty rather than hand use, and then, with Fiona's permission, rearranged it to
something uglier and faster.

When Fiona saw the first stable green text rendered from a distant store, she
did not speak. She simply stood behind the operator's chair with one hand on the
back rail and watched the letters bloom.

Faraday, older now, stood beside her.

"You look disappointed," he said.

"No," Fiona answered after a moment. "Only late."

He did not understand that, which was probably for the best.

The protocols that made the system useful were not grand. They were,
characteristically, clerical:

- addresses
- route headers
- checksums
- retransmit requests
- queue priorities
- file identifiers
- access classes

Around these grew culture. Operators developed preferences and prejudices.
Scientific offices preferred terse symbolic traffic. Merchants preferred
verbosity where money might later deny intent. Government departments preferred
everything stamped with hierarchy. Young people, as they always do when given a
new medium, found ways to use it incorrectly with remarkable creativity.

The first unauthorized social note passed through the system on the fourth day.
It was, disappointingly, not political but romantic. A junior routing clerk sent
three lines of reckless sentiment to a woman in a stores office under cover of a
fault query. The woman replied with a correction to his spelling and an answer
that was, from a systems point of view, encouraging. The network was alive
enough to be misused.

Ada, seeing one terminal room in person not long before illness began taking too
much of her strength, placed her fingertips lightly against the casing and said,
"It is not the engine one imagines first. It is the conversation."

Fiona turned to look at her.

"Yes."

"You have built a republic of correspondents."

"No," Fiona said. "Only the possibility of one."

Ada smiled with the sadness of the very intelligent. "That is generally the
largest thing anyone builds."

By then the network extended beyond what any one office could entirely know.
Britain, parts of the Continent, selected imperial routes, linked storehouses,
calculation centers, relay exchanges, and administrative terminals. International
in the nineteenth-century sense: not universal, not free, not equal, but real.
Small file transfer existed. Electronic mail existed. Multi-megabyte storage
existed if one counted the House of Memory and its provincial mirrors. Green
screens existed. The world had not become `1980`. It had become something that
would have made `1980` feel uncomfortably close.

And yet people remained themselves.

Governments censored.
Merchants speculated.
Radicals encrypted badly.
Lovers sent dangerous notes.
Bureaucrats invented delay in systems designed to abolish it.

No machine had corrected that architecture.

Why should it?
