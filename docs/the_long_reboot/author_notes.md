# The Long Reboot - Author Notes

## Premise

Fiona, a `2026` medical device engineer, is thrown without explanation into
`1800` Britain with roughly `20 kg` of luggage and a modern engineer's memory.
Her goal is not merely survival. She decides, slowly and reluctantly, to
restart the technical trajectory of the world as fast as she can.

The project is not framed as "invent the transistor early". It is framed as
building the industrial, scientific, and political substrate required to reach
something like `1980` information technology within a single lifetime:

- green-screen terminals
- long-distance message networks
- small file transfer
- multi-megabyte storage
- institutional computing

## Tone

- grounded alternate history
- technical but readable aloud
- political and social consequences matter
- occasional drama is welcome, but avoid fantasy drift
- minor romance only, not central
- ending should remain ambiguous in first draft

## Structural Intent

- one markdown file per chapter
- chapters should be suitable for podcast reading
- continuity should be tracked here so the draft can resume after interruption

## Fiona

### Background

- born in late twentieth/early twenty-first century Britain
- active in `2026`
- medical device engineer
- strong in:
  - systems thinking
  - reliability
  - instrumentation
  - documentation discipline
  - regulatory mindset
  - practical electronics
- not omniscient
- understands enough chemistry, electronics, manufacturing, networking, and
  physiology to guide specialists, but cannot personally substitute for an
  industrial civilization

### Character

- pragmatic
- observant
- not a revolutionary by temperament
- becomes one by circumstance
- uneasy with power
- understands that every "improvement" has military and political uses

## Luggage

Base assumption:

- around `20 kg`, but can stretch modestly if still human-portable

Contents should be high leverage:

- notebooks with hand-written and printed technical notes
- compact solar panel / charger
- power banks
- two rugged consumer devices with offline library copies
- measuring tools:
  - calipers
  - micrometer
  - loupe
- selected modern components:
  - LEDs
  - diodes
  - transistors
  - quartz parts
- stationery and drafting tools

## Historical Frame

Starting point:

- Britain in `1800`
- Napoleonic era background
- industrialization beginning but incomplete
- precision tooling, chemistry, electrification, and communications are all
  immature enough to make Fiona's knowledge decisive if she can survive

## Candidate Historical Figures

These are available to introduce later if useful:

- Humphry Davy
- Michael Faraday
- Charles Babbage
- Ada Lovelace
- Isambard Kingdom Brunel
- Joseph Banks
- Napoleon Bonaparte
- Alexander von Humboldt
- Carl Friedrich Gauss

Ada Lovelace should appear if chronology allows. She is a bonus figure, not a
mandatory early character.

## Long Arc

Phase arc:

1. survival, credibility, patronage
2. workshop culture, metrology, telegraphy
3. chemistry, batteries, dynamos, communications network
4. vacuum tubes, magnetic recording, radio
5. computation, terminals, international message exchange

The heart of the novel is not just invention but institution-building.

## Thematic Questions

- What does one person owe the future?
- What happens when technical acceleration outruns social adaptation?
- Can progress be guided, or only redirected?
- Does Fiona improve the world, militarize it earlier, or both?

## Continuity Notes

### Chapter 1

- title: `The Weight of Twenty Kilograms`
- Fiona arrives in rural England in `1800`
- no explanation for time travel
- opening focus on immediate physical reality: weather, clothing, weight,
  shock, inventory, and the mismatch between theory and survival
- she decides early that the real bottleneck is not knowledge but machine tools
- chapter ends with her first glimpse of a road and the first human voices,
  with the sense that history has just become dangerously local

### Chapter 2

- title: `A Respectable Story`
- Fiona is helped by three local men with a damaged cart
- she reaches a village inn and confirms the year is `1800`
- she tells practical lies to remain safe:
  - stranded
  - fallen from respectable circumstances
- she has no useful money
- she earns temporary shelter by applying modern hygiene and practical medical
  reasoning to an injured child
- she sees how class, sex, and reputation shape every conversation
- she realises London and scientific patronage are the only viable route
- chapter ends with Fiona deciding she must construct a socially believable
  identity before she can begin constructing the future

### Chapter 3

- title: `Patronage or Prison`
- village scrutiny hardens overnight
- Fiona is examined by local authority figures:
  - innkeeper
  - clergyman
  - magistrate or land agent
- her education makes her suspicious rather than respectable
- she uses practical demonstration instead of prophecy:
  - fixes or improves a local technical problem
  - likely a pump, lock, or wheel repair diagnosis
- a written letter is proposed to pass her onward toward Cambridge
- she sees clearly that every patron will also be a potential jailer
- chapter ends with her leaving the village under escort or recommendation,
  not yet free but no longer static

### Chapter 4

- title: `The Letter to Cambridge`
- Fiona reaches Cambridge under recommendation
- she learns to survive by being useful, restrained, and legible to patrons
- academic curiosity and social distrust coexist
- Fiona begins choosing which truths to spend and which to hide
- chapter ends with her first credible route toward London scientific circles

### Chapter 5

- title: `Men of Science`
- Fiona enters London technical culture through patronage
- Davy and associated institutions become early anchors
- she establishes herself less as a prophet than as a systems-minded practical
  mechanic and organizer
- tension between spectacle science and disciplined engineering is introduced

### Chapter 6

- title: `The First Wire`
- early signaling work turns into the first real network experiments
- Fiona pushes standards, maintenance, and procedure rather than isolated
  invention
- commercial and state interests begin to see communications as infrastructure

### Chapter 7

- title: `Mr. Faraday of Blandford Street`
- Faraday enters as a central working intellect and laboratory force
- the novel shifts from lone knowledge to team knowledge
- Fiona finds in Faraday someone who understands disciplined experiment rather
  than merely dramatic effect

### Chapter 8

- title: `Peace, War, and the Uses of Speed`
- the network becomes political
- faster communication strengthens both administration and military planning
- Fiona understands that every technical gain produces surveillance and control
  opportunities

### Chapter 9

- title: `Brass and Error`
- Babbage enters through the problem of error, procedure, and repeatability
- Fiona's emphasis on systems, tooling, and maintainability intersects with
  Babbage's ambition
- the novel broadens from communications into computation

### Chapter 10

- title: `The Enchantress of Number`
- Ada Lovelace appears and becomes an intellectual bridge figure
- symbol, procedure, and abstract instruction become explicit themes
- Fiona sees that the future depends on notation and culture as much as
  hardware

### Chapter 11

- title: `Fire in Glass`
- vacuum work, tubes, and display technology begin to mature
- Fiona confronts how much industrial patience is hidden inside "simple"
  electronic progress

### Chapter 12

- title: `The House of Memory`
- magnetic storage and institutional archives come into focus
- the system becomes less about individual brilliance and more about durable
  organizational memory

### Chapter 13

- title: `Green Letters`
- recognizable 1980-class terminal culture appears
- green text screens, routed messages, and routine technical exchange become
  lived reality
- the accomplishment is functionally modern even if physically large and
  socially uneven

### Chapter 14

- title: `The Long Reboot`
- set in `1848`
- Europe is networked enough that unrest, administration, commerce, and science
  all move with unprecedented speed
- Fiona is elderly and institutionalized as a figure of authority but no longer
  controls what she built
- the ending is ambiguous:
  - the world is transformed
  - the technology worked
  - its moral consequences remain unresolved
