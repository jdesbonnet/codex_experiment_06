# Chapter 14: The Long Reboot

In `1848`, Europe caught fire in many cities at once.

In the old history Fiona remembered, the revolutions had moved by pamphlet,
rhetoric, hunger, rumor, and delay. In this one they moved by those means as
well, but not only by those means. Messages outran horses. Provincial offices
received instructions before crowds arrived. Crowds learned what had happened in
other capitals before governments could agree upon a version fit for publication.
Files crossed borders. So did lies.

The network had not caused the century's unrest. It had simply removed some of
the friction that had once localized it.

Fiona was old enough by then to be treated, in rooms of consequence, as part
woman, part institution. Her hair had silvered. Her hands hurt in damp weather.
Younger engineers spoke of "Marsh standards" in the mild resentment reserved for
rules that had saved enough disasters to become annoying. She had outlived
Davy. She had not outlived Faraday, not yet, but both of them walked more
carefully than once they had. Ada, gone too soon, remained in the notation of
half the system and in the habits of thought of people who had never met her.

On an evening in October she sat in a control room above the Thames, wrapped in
a plain shawl over an old black dress, while operators moved quietly between
terminal positions and switching desks. Outside, fog pressed against the windows
and made the city feel close and borderless. Inside, green text flickered across
glass.

Paris.
Brussels.
Edinburgh.
Trieste.
Calcutta by a chain of routes and delays that still, by the standards of old
empires, counted as miraculous.

Not instantaneous. Not universal. But enough.

A young operator handed her a printed summary with the awkward respect of
someone raised on stories in which the elderly woman by the window had always
been present, like a portrait that answered back.

"Traffic is rising on the continental circuits, ma'am."

Ma'am. She had once been Miss Marsh with suspicious boots. Now she was ma'am,
which was in its way equally unreal.

"Political?" she asked.

"Some. Commercial more. Scientific traffic from Paris reduced. Manchester asks
whether routing tables should be revised for overnight priority."

"They always ask that when frightened."

The operator allowed himself the smallest smile.

She dismissed him and looked at the summary.

Every line on the page was something that had not needed to exist. Address
headers. Store references. Queue identifiers. A notation for attached
procedures. A checksum field. Clean little bureaucratic miracles.

She thought of the lane in Suffolk. Of Mrs. Davy's kitchen. Of Haines in
Cambridge holding the caliper like a relic. Of Davy in the battery room. Of
Faraday in the rain. Of Ada saying the engine was not the thing but the
conversation.

It had all worked.

That was the terrible part.

Not perfectly. Not safely. Not morally. But enough.

England had not become gentle because it had become fast. Europe had not become
wise because it had become connected. Empire had learned coordination. So had
resistance. Administrators had gained memory. So had dissenters. Information had
become harder to suppress and easier to centralize, often in the same hour.

Fiona stood and walked slowly along the row of screens.

At one station, a message from Paris was being decoded and routed. At another, a
merchant request from Glasgow waited in queue behind government traffic. At a
third, a technical file from a laboratory in Geneva had failed validation on one
symbol and been kicked back automatically with a fault notice so terse and
familiar it might have come from her own lost century.

She put her fingertips lightly against the cool metal frame.

In another world, perhaps, she might have considered this triumph.

In this one she thought it looked more like responsibility with no final audit.

The night supervisor approached.

"There is a question from the Atlantic committee," he said.

Of course there was. There was always an Atlantic committee now. If Europe could
be made electrical, why not the ocean? Why not bind two worlds with copper,
protocol, and stubbornness? Merchants wanted it. Governments wanted it. Men of
vision wanted it for different and usually less honest reasons.

"What question?" Fiona asked.

"Whether, if the cable succeeds, the western offices should mirror the London
stores in full."

She almost laughed. After half a century, the questions had become so elegantly
modern.

"Tell them no," she said. "Then tell them to ask me again tomorrow with better
reasons."

The supervisor nodded and withdrew.

Fiona remained where she was while the screens pulsed softly in the dim room.
From the river came the muted sound of traffic and foghorns. Behind her, papers
moved, relays clicked, valves hummed, and the operators kept the republic of
signals alive by the oldest method in history: attentive labor.

She had come, years ago, wanting to rebuild the world as quickly as possible.
That phrase now struck her as young. Worlds did not reboot. They accumulated.
They bent. They carried old violence into new architectures and called the
result progress if enough of it glowed.

On the nearest screen a fresh line appeared from Paris.

`REQUEST PRIORITY SCIENTIFIC ROUTE.`

Below it, from Manchester:

`TRADE TRAFFIC DELAY UNACCEPTABLE.`

Below that, from an internal government office:

`CONFIRM ARCHIVE ACCESS CLASS REVISION.`

Three futures, all arriving at once.

Fiona looked at them until the words blurred slightly with age and fatigue.

Then she reached past the operator, set her hand near the keyboard, and typed a
single routing instruction of her own.

Not because she still controlled the network. She did not. That had been true
for years.

But because, now and then, history still allowed its earliest engineer a small
editorial privilege.

When she stepped away, the green letters held a moment longer on the glass
before passing on into the system she had helped create and could no longer
fully see.

Outside, the fog thickened over London.

Inside, the future remained lit.
