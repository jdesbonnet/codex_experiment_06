# Chapter 2: A Respectable Story

The man on the cart looked first at Fiona, then at the case, then at the lane
behind her, as if a fourth explanation might emerge if he inspected the road
hard enough.

"Help with what?" he said.

He was older than the other two by twenty years at least, thick through the
chest, with a face reddened by weather and a neck sunk into his coat as if cold
were a permanent argument to be borne rather than won. The younger men waited
without speaking. One had straw in his hair and the wary expression of somebody
accustomed to blame. The other was scarcely more than a boy.

Fiona had already made one useful calculation: they were curious before they
were hostile. That was not safety, but it was time.

"I'm afraid I've lost my way," she said.

The older man glanced once more at the empty lane.

"Aye," he said, "I can see that much."

The younger of the two standing by the wheel gave a brief snort and looked down
at once, ashamed of laughing in front of her or in front of the older man,
perhaps both.

"Is there a village near?" Fiona asked.

"There is if you keep to the road and do not spring out of hedges," the older
man said. "What village d'you want?"

That was the first dangerous question. Fiona could not name a place she did not
know and she did not yet know enough to choose one that belonged naturally in
her mouth.

"Whichever is nearest," she said. "If there is an inn."

"An inn," he repeated, and looked at her clothes with renewed attention. Not
the kind of stare a man gives a woman when he is thinking of her as a woman.
The much colder stare he gives an object that might belong to somebody richer
than himself and therefore might later cause paperwork.

"You've no horse," he said. "No maid. No servant. No coach. And no road on this
side leads to anywhere worth losing a lady."

It was not exactly a question, which made it harder.

Fiona bent to the case as if to lift it again, buying a second to think.

"I am not a lady," she said.

That, at least, was true in every sense that mattered here.

The older man grunted, unconvinced. "No?"

"No."

"Then you're very well taught for something else."

The boy on the shaft spoke for the first time. "Maybe she come off the London
road."

"Maybe she fell from the moon," the older man said. "Hold your tongue, Ned."

The younger labourer by the wheel had been staring at Fiona's boots. "Them are
queer."

Fiona resisted the urge to hide them under the hem of her coat.

"Imported," she said, because the lie offered itself fully formed and because
there was no world in which this place could verify it.

The older man gave her a long look. "Well then, Miss Imported, we are bound to
Little Abberton, and Little Abberton has an inn if Mrs. Davy has not at last
driven her husband to murder for economy. If you can walk, you may walk with
us. If you cannot, Ned can ride and you can perch on the shaft. The box goes in
back."

He spoke as though he had already decided.

Fiona let out the breath she had been holding.

"Thank you."

"Thank me if we reach there dry."

He stepped down from the cart with the caution of a man who knew exactly how
much his knees cost him each morning and went to the rear. The younger labourer
joined him. Between them they lifted the case. Both men grunted at the weight.

"God preserve us," said the younger one. "What's in it?"

"Books," Fiona said.

That won her the first useful silence.

Books, in 2026, were self-justifying clutter. Books, here, were either wealth,
religion, or trouble. Possibly all three.

The older man shoved the case into the cart and wiped his hands on his coat.

"Tom," he said to the younger labourer, "mind the wheel. Ned, off. Miss, if you
please."

Fiona climbed awkwardly onto the shaft while the boy slid down, and the cart
set off again with a groan from the damaged side. The lane ran between hedges
beaded with cold water. Mud struck the spokes. The horse smelled of wet hide
and patient resignation.

For several minutes no one spoke. Fiona used the time to steady herself by the
old method of counting facts.

The horse tack was well used but competently kept. The cart itself had a worn
iron tyre on the bad wheel and a slight wobble that suggested not a shattered
axle but looseness in the assembly. The men's clothes were coarse and patched
but not ragged. She could hear no church bell. The air tasted maritime in the
far distance or perhaps simply flat; East Anglia remained plausible.

The older man walked near her knee, one gloved hand on the horse's bridle.

"You have a name?" he asked at last.

She had expected that sooner.

Names were dangerous. Her own felt too modern, though only just. But to invent
a full false self in three seconds was to invite disaster later.

"Fiona," she said.

He glanced up. "Fiona what?"

"Marsh."

The surname came from nowhere and immediately acquired the terrible authority
of anything spoken when one is frightened.

"Marsh," he said. "And where do you belong, Miss Marsh?"

That question, Fiona suspected, would pursue her for years.

"Cambridgeshire," she said.

It was close enough to her old life to answer naturally and far enough to be
useful. Regional belonging in England could stretch, particularly if one had
the right vowels and sufficient confidence.

"Can you prove it?" he asked mildly.

She looked down at him. "Can you prove I must?"

For the first time his expression changed. Not warmer, but interested.

"Tom," he said after a moment, still looking at her, "did I not say she was
well taught?"

"You said she was trouble."

"The one often follows."

They came at last to houses: first a cottage with a low roof and smoke seeping
sideways from the chimney, then a smithy, then a scatter of buildings gathered
around what could be called a village only by the standards of those with no
choice but to call it one. Little Abberton announced itself less by sign than
by compactness. Human life drawn in upon itself against winter.

People looked up as the cart went by.

Fiona felt their eyes on the case before she felt them on her face. Whatever
she looked like, the luggage made her a story.

The inn stood at a bend where the road widened just enough for a hitching post
and a trough. The sign above the door had once been painted with something
heraldic and now resembled a weather-beaten argument between a lion and a mop.
Inside, when the door opened, there came a wave of warmth, yeast, wet wool, and
human proximity that almost made her dizzy.

The older man nodded toward it. "The Crown."

"Do I ask for Mrs. Davy or the lion?" Fiona said before she could stop herself.

Tom barked a laugh. The older man gave her the briefest shadow of approval.

"Ask for whichever looks less murderous."

The room beyond the threshold was low-beamed and crowded with small signs of
use: tankards, boots drying near the hearth, a dog that lifted its head and
declined further interest, a woman in a brown gown cutting bread with the
economy of somebody who had done it too long to waste motion. She turned as
they entered and took in the tableau in a single sweep: cartmen, mud, stranger,
case.

"No," she said at once.

"We have not yet asked," said the older man.

"Then I refuse efficiently."

He removed his hat. "Mrs. Davy, this is Miss Marsh, who has lost her road and
requires a room."

"Then she had best ask at a better inn."

Mrs. Davy was perhaps fifty, though Fiona knew well enough that hard lives
aged people beyond arithmetic. Her face was broad, clever, and entirely
unfooled.

"I can pay," Fiona said.

Mrs. Davy's eyes dropped at once to Fiona's hands, then to the absence of any
reticule, purse, servant, or visible means of producing coin.

"Can you."

Fiona's pockets held a house key, a folded shopping receipt, half a packet of
mints, and a modern debit card useful to no one in any century without the rest
of the civilization attached.

"Not immediately," she said.

"Then no."

The refusal was not cruel. That made it worse. It was simply a calculation.
Fuel, bread, sheets, risk.

Ned, the boy, had already drifted toward the hearth where another, smaller boy
sat on a stool with his hand bundled in linen. Fiona saw the dressing first in
the way trained people see a problem before they know they've looked at it: too
thick, too dry, and dark where fresh seepage had crept through. The child was
trying not to cry. That effort alone told her the pain was bad.

Mrs. Davy saw Fiona looking and moved half a step, instinctively between them.

"My son has no business that concerns you."

"No," Fiona said. "But that bandage does."

The room changed.

It was not dramatic. No one gasped. But everyone became still in the precise
way people become still when a stranger ceases to be merely inconvenient and
becomes potentially useful or dangerous.

Mrs. Davy's expression hardened. "Are you a surgeon?"

"No."

"A physician?"

"No."

"Then I think—"

"I think," Fiona said, more sharply than intended, "that if the hand was
burned, or torn, or crushed, then keeping it wrapped in old linen beside a fire
will do it no kindness."

She had no desire to play doctor. She had spent enough of her real life
fighting clinicians, regulators, and procurement departments to know exactly
where her competence ended. But she also knew wound hygiene, sterility as a
discipline, and the basic brutal arithmetic of infection. The industrial
centuries had taught many things badly; they had taught sepsis thoroughly.

Mrs. Davy lifted her chin. "And you know this how?"

Fiona looked at the child. Ten, perhaps. Pale around the mouth. Sweat at the
hairline though the room was only moderately warm. That could still be pain
rather than fever.

"Because I have seen what happens when dirt is sealed in," she said. "Because
the linen should be clean. Because the water should be boiled. Because whoever
changed it last should have washed first."

There was a silence long enough for offence to settle in it.

Then, from the far side of the hearth, a man Fiona had not noticed said, "She
is not wrong."

He was thin, grey at the temples, with the stooped shoulders of someone who sat
too long over accounts or small craft. Not a doctor. Perhaps simply a man with
some memory of bad outcomes. He kept his eyes on the child.

Mrs. Davy turned on him. "Are you taking her side in my own house, Elias?"

So the older man from the cart was Elias. It suited him.

"I am saying she is not wrong," he repeated.

Mrs. Davy's mouth tightened. She looked at Fiona again, now not as a vagrant
but as a nuisance of a more expensive kind.

"If I let you near him and you worsen it, I will have you turned out into the
dark before the hour is done."

"If you do not let me look," Fiona said, "then nothing I say matters and you
lose only the satisfaction of refusing me."

Tom coughed into his hand to hide a smile.

Mrs. Davy hesitated.

That hesitation was all Fiona needed. She crossed the room slowly, keeping her
hands visible. The boy watched her with the grave distrust children reserve for
adults discussing them as if they were furniture.

"What's your name?" she asked him.

"Will."

"Does it hurt?"

"No," he said instantly, which meant yes.

Fiona crouched. "May I see?"

He looked to his mother. Mrs. Davy gave one curt nod.

The linen smelled faintly of fat and smoke. Fiona unwound it carefully. The
hand beneath was angry red across the palm and lower fingers, with blistering
broken in one place and a crust of whatever domestic salve had been used first.
A kitchen burn, perhaps. Bad, but not yet catastrophic.

She exhaled without letting it show.

"He needs the hand cooled and cleaned," she said. "Not cold enough to freeze
him. Just cool water. Clean cloth. Fresh each time."

"We did that," Mrs. Davy said defensively.

"Then do it again. And boil the cloth first."

"Boil linen?"

"Yes."

That won a look from everyone in the room.

"It will shrink," Mrs. Davy said.

"Then better a smaller cloth than a dead child."

She regretted the phrase as soon as it was out, but only as rhetoric. Not as
truth.

No one answered.

Fiona became aware of her own hands, clean by modern standards only because she
had cleaned them that morning in another century. Not clean enough. She wanted
soap, boiling water, and gloves. She had none. The whole room suddenly seemed
made of contamination: table edges, sleeves, smoke residue, the grain of wood
polished by generations of touch.

"Boiling water," she said more quietly. "And if you have spirits, use some to
clean the skin around, not in, the broken part. Then dry cloth. Loose. Not
tight."

"And for the pain?" Will asked in a very small voice.

Fiona almost laughed, not because it was funny but because the question was
modern in its fairness. Adults could talk all they liked about methods. The
patient still wanted to know the useful part.

"It will hurt less if it is clean," she said. "And if you keep it from the
fire."

"You hear that?" said Mrs. Davy, half to the boy, half to the room.

But the edge had gone out of her tone. She was thinking now not about class or
insolence but about whether the stranger might be right.

Fiona rewrapped the hand as best she could and stood.

"That is all I can say without proper supplies."

"Proper supplies," Mrs. Davy repeated.

"Soap. Clean linen. Needles. Better instruments than kitchens keep."

"You speak as if all the world ought to be a surgery."

Fiona thought of clean rooms, autoclavable trays, controlled packaging,
disposable drapes, sterility indicators, traceability records, post-market
surveillance, and a thousand miracles so normalized they no longer looked like
miracles to anyone who had not been deprived of them.

"No," she said. "Only the parts where blood opens."

Mrs. Davy studied her for a long time.

"You said you can pay."

"Eventually."

"That is not payment."

"No."

"But you may sleep above the kitchen tonight," Mrs. Davy said, each word
clearly costing her some inward concession. "Not in one of the proper rooms.
And if in the morning you prove a thief or a lunatic, I shall consider the
charity badly invested."

Fiona inclined her head. "That is fair."

"It is not charity," Mrs. Davy snapped. "It is barter. There is dignity in one
and waste in the other."

That, Fiona thought, might be the first sentence from 1800 she entirely loved.

The room resumed movement in cautious increments. Elias and Tom returned to the
cart. Ned followed them. Mrs. Davy sent for water. Will stared at Fiona as if
she had stepped out of a book his mother would not let him read.

Only when she was left briefly alone by the hearth did Fiona allow her knees to
shake.

She had a bed for one night. That was all.

By evening she had learned three useful things. First, the year was indeed
`1800`, confirmed by an almanac nailed near the settle and a newspaper two days
old whose talk of Parliament, trade, and France pinned her to the opening of a
century that had not yet decided how violent it intended to be. Second, she was
in Suffolk, not Cambridgeshire, which meant her guess had been close enough to
be lucky but wrong enough to be dangerous if repeated. Third, Little Abberton
was the sort of place where everyone knew, by suppertime, that an educated
woman with unnatural boots and no money was sleeping over Mrs. Davy's kitchen.

By the time the common room thinned and the shutters were barred, Fiona had
constructed the beginnings of a respectable story.

She was not married. Better to be a dependent relation than a wife without a
husband. She had connections in Cambridge. Better a county gentry adjacency
than London, where details multiplied. She had been traveling under strained
circumstances. True enough. She had books because she had been unusually well
educated by a father who held uncommon views. Also plausible. The box contained
papers and instruments. Dangerous, but unavoidable now that its weight had been
remarked upon.

The one thing she could not satisfactorily invent was money.

Money was not only coin. It was proof that one's story had been believed by
someone before. She had none of that accumulated trust. No references, no
letters, no family names that would open doors, no handwriting already known to
another hand.

When Mrs. Davy showed her at last to the room above the kitchen, it was smaller
than the word room ordinarily implied: a narrow bed, a washstand, a single
candle, plaster cracked by age, and the warmth of the flue working its way
through one wall. Fiona set the case down and sat on the bed in her clothes.

For the first time since arriving, she let herself think beyond the next hour.

London.

There was no realistic alternative.

Whatever England of `1800` might permit a lone woman to do on her own, it would
not be enough. The future she carried in her head could not be traded village
by village like lace or patent tonics. To make any difference at all she would
need men with money, rooms full of instruments, printers, chemists, iron,
glass, and apprentices. She would need patrons before she needed disciples.

Joseph Banks, perhaps, if she could reach anyone near him. The Royal
Institution. Humphry Davy before he became marble. People who still believed
experiments could rearrange the nation rather than merely decorate it.

The scale of it made her almost laugh again.

She was lying in a narrow bed above a kitchen, with sore hands and no proper
currency, planning the accelerated industrialization of the Western world.

Her profession had not, strictly speaking, prepared her for that.

And yet the shape of the problem was familiar. Every hard project she had ever
worked on had begun with the same embarrassment: reality refusing to align with
the neat stack diagram. There was always some missing interface, some filthy
constraint, some unglamorous subsystem on which the grand design actually
depended.

Here, the missing subsystem was society.

She took out the smallest notebook again and wrote by candlelight:

`Requirements:`

`1. food`

`2. money`

`3. identity`

`4. patrons`

Then, after a moment:

`5. machine tools`

She stared at the list until the candle guttered.

Identity belonged above patrons because no one funded a person who could not be
explained to their own servants.

That, more than hunger, frightened her. She could sketch a galvanic pile from
memory. She could explain feedback, sterilization, standard screw threads,
electromagnetic relays, error-checking in digital transmission. But before any
of that she had to solve a much older engineering problem: how to build a
socially credible self from inadequate materials.

Down below, she could hear the murmur of Mrs. Davy's voice, the scrape of a pot
lid, the closing of a door. Then the house settled around her in the old way:
timber, ash, and breathing.

Fiona lay down without undressing and put one hand on the case.

The bag was still too heavy.

But now, for the first time, she understood what else it was.

Not luggage. Not really.

A prototype.

Too dense, badly packaged, underfunded, and not yet fit for field conditions,
but containing within it the first clumsy version of everything that might
follow.

Below her, in the dark and the heat of the kitchen wall, a child slept with a
cleaner bandage than he might otherwise have had. Outside, somewhere beyond the
village, the road continued south toward larger roads, larger rooms, and men
who mistook patronage for ownership.

In the morning she would need better lies.

Not wild ones. Respectable ones.

The sort that could pass through a parlour door without being announced as
fraud.

Only then, Fiona thought, eyes open in the dark, could she begin the more
difficult business of telling the truth.
