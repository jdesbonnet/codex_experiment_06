# Chapter 5: Men of Science

London smelled of horses, drains, coal, cheap food, expensive perfume, and the
collective insistence of too many people making too many arrangements in too
little space. Fiona had lived in cities all her adult life and had forgotten
how much of urban civilization depended on invisible infrastructure. London in
1800 possessed the crowd without the concealment.

Cresswell lodged them in Bloomsbury in rooms chosen for affordability and moral
defensibility. The Royal Institution, when Fiona first saw it, disappointed her
in exactly the right way. It was not yet a myth. Only a building in which
wealthy men and curious women came to admire useful spectacle and congratulate
themselves on valuing inquiry.

That was enough. Institutions did not need purity. Only resources.

The demonstration they were invited to observe was chemical, fashionable, and
slightly theatrical. Humphry Davy, younger than Fiona had imagined and far more
beautiful in motion than any man had a right to be while speaking of gases,
held the room with an ease she recognized instantly as dangerous. He was not
merely intelligent. He was persuasive in real time, which was an altogether
different threat.

Afterward, in a side chamber smelling of acid, wax, and warm metal, they were
kept waiting just long enough to understand the favor of not being forgotten.

Banks did not appear. A secretary did, and with him Davy himself, removing his
gloves with quick, graceful impatience.

"Miss Marsh," he said, glancing from Cresswell's face to hers and correctly
identifying the source of the trouble. "Mr. Cresswell tells me you have
interests in mechanics, chemistry, and electricity. This is either admirable or
impossible."

"It has certainly been inconvenient," Fiona said.

Davy laughed at that and waved them to chairs. The secretary remained standing,
which told her more about power than any introduction could have.

"You understand," Davy said, "that many people write to this place claiming
discoveries."

"I imagine many people have discovered that gentlemen of science are easier to
interest than gentlemen of finance."

"You imagine correctly. It is one of our defects."

He sat opposite her, all brightness and attention. Fiona felt, with some
annoyance, that he was doing the same rapid diagnosis she herself used in
laboratories: competence, vanity, instability, usefulness, risk. Human
factors, only in a better coat.

"Very well," he said. "Convince me not that you are learned, which can be
faked, but that you know where learning runs aground."

There it was. A real question.

Fiona had prepared speeches on galvanic efficiency, relay logic, chemical
purification, and standards. She discarded them all.

"Measurement," she said. "Repeatability. Bad screws. Worse glass. Impure copper.
Acids too expensive. Seals that leak. Men who call apparatus unreliable when
they mean they cannot make the same thing twice."

The secretary's face did not change, but Davy's did.

"Go on."

"You may have brilliant experiments every day for ten years and still build
nothing of lasting use if every improvement remains a curiosity in one room.
England needs instrument makers more than lecturers, and standards more than
genius."

Davy leaned back. "You take a severe view of genius."

"Only because I have seen what it costs when manufacturing is treated as a
servant rather than a partner."

She saw then that she had him, not because he agreed, but because she had
offended him in the correct register.

"And what," he asked, "would you do first, if this nation were absurd enough to
place means at your disposal?"

Fiona answered without pause.

"Machine screws. Gauges. Better pumps. Better batteries. Better insulation. A
workshop attached to every laboratory. Drawings kept by rule, not memory.
Electrical signaling for distance. Practical chemistry, not only performative
chemistry."

"Performative?"

"For audiences," she said. "Not outcomes."

The secretary coughed lightly into one hand. Davy's mouth twitched.

"You are impertinent, Miss Marsh."

"Yes."

"And possibly useful. Which is the greater fault, I have yet to determine."

The interview lasted an hour. Davy pressed, parried, tested. He asked about
voltaic piles and metals, medical applications, gas, drainage, corrosion,
magnetism, practical workshop organization, and why she believed signaling over
distance would matter beyond novelty.

"Because governments will pay for it first," Fiona said, "and merchants will
pay for it second. Once they do, everyone else must live in the world it
creates."

"You speak of it as inevitable."

"If it works, it is."

At the end, Davy rose and walked once the length of the room, hands behind his
back.

"Mr. Cresswell," he said, "your unusual young lady is not a fraud in any simple
sense."

"I took that to be the case."

"Then I shall disappoint both charity and prudence. Miss Marsh, I can offer no
formal position. Yet. But there is a cabinet room below stairs, a bench, some
apparatus, and a chronic shortage of people who understand that a laboratory is
also a workshop. If you are willing to assist with practical trials, and if you
are content for the arrangement to be... imprecisely described, we may continue
this conversation."

Imprecisely described.

That meant no rank, no salary worth naming, no formal acknowledgment, and every
later dispute tilted by ambiguity.

It was still more than she had hoped for.

"On what terms?" she asked.

Davy smiled, this time not as a performer but as a duelist offered a better
sword.

"The institution will provide lodgings of the plainest description, a modest
stipend, and materials as justified. In return you will be useful, discreet,
and wrong as seldom as possible."

"That seems fair."

"It is not," said the secretary, speaking for the first time. "It is simply the
best available."

Fiona liked him immediately and trusted him not at all.

So began the next life.

The rooms allotted to her were smaller than the Bloomsbury lodgings and vastly
more valuable. Below the lecture rooms and demonstration spaces, beyond the
territories of servants and supply, lay a world she understood in the body even
before she knew its layout: benches scarred by use, shelves crowded with glass,
furnaces, balances, boxes of wire, tools not properly catalogued, acids whose
containers offended her on sight, batteries assembled with elegance and
improvised with equal confidence.

The Royal Institution did not need invention from her first. It needed
discipline.

Within a month she had introduced, quietly and without naming it as reform:

- labeled storage for critical parts
- serial numbering for experimental assemblies
- notebooks with fixed headings
- material logs
- simple go/no-go gauges in Haines's style
- cleaning routines that reduced contamination and breakage

Davy mocked some of it as "domestic tyranny" until he discovered that the same
apparatus could be rebuilt twice without argument. After that he called it
"method" and explained it to visitors as though it had arisen naturally from
his own temperament.

Fiona did not object. Credit, in 1800, was less important than control of the
next step.

The next step came by way of batteries.

The voltaic pile fascinated everyone and served no one nearly as well as it
might. It leaked, corroded, failed irregularly, and behaved like a spoiled
relative. Fiona could not conjure modern cells, but she could see immediately
that much of the misery came from practical neglect: contact surfaces, fluid
management, sealing, repeatable assembly, the inability to compare one build to
another except by mood and anecdote.

She and Davy argued for three evenings over plate arrangements and electrolyte
handling until he at last slammed down a pair of tongs and said, "Very well,
Miss Marsh, let us see whether your contempt for slovenliness can produce a
better current than my enthusiasm."

It could.

Not miraculous. Not modern. But enough better, and enough more stable, to
justify repetition. That was the thing almost everyone in every century
underestimated: improvement compounded less by brilliance than by reliability.

With better cells came better electromagnets. With better electromagnets came
the first conversations, still private, about signaling.

Davy cared because it was interesting. The War Office cared because it was
useful. Banks, once he finally took notice, cared because any system that moved
information faster altered the Empire's shape.

Fiona cared because wire was the first honest road to the future.

One wet evening, leaving the Institution by a side door after midnight, she
paused under the portico and watched the lamps glisten on the street.

She had gained what she needed and feared most: a foothold.

In her pocket was a list of required materials for line trials. On her desk
upstairs lay the first careful sketches of insulated signaling circuits simple
enough to build with this century's hands. Somewhere in the building Davy was
still awake, because men like him mistook exhaustion for momentum and often
profited by it.

London rolled around them, enormous and badly managed and alive.

For the first time since the lane, Fiona allowed herself a dangerous thought.

This might work.
