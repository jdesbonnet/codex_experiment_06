# Chapter 8: Peace, War, and the Uses of Speed

Peace did not save the wire from war. It only changed the uniforms around it.

By `1815`, as Europe exhausted itself and governments began pretending surprise
at the price of their own ambitions, the signaling network had already escaped
its original justifications. Admiralty dispatches moved through it. Treasury
figures moved through it. So did private commercial instructions disguised as
urgencies of national interest and national urgencies disguised as accounting.

Fiona had thought, in her first cold days, that the challenge would be to make
people believe in electrical coordination at all. She had not anticipated how
quickly belief would outrun prudence.

The first serious confrontation came not with Davy or Banks but with a Home
Office undersecretary named Lethbridge, a man with tidy whiskers, dry cuffs,
and the unnerving habit of speaking to every person in a room as though they
were two levels below the one at which his true thoughts were kept.

He visited the network office on a grey morning in late autumn and was shown
the message books, routing tables, battery logs, and maintenance records with
the satisfaction of a man expecting to praise orderly innovation.

Instead, he asked a simpler question.

"Who may send?"

Fiona answered before Davy could.

"At present, those with lines, agreements, and the means to pay."

"That is not an answer. I ask who may send without review."

There was the state, stripped down to essentials.

Davy gave one of his smoother replies about practical administration and mutual
interest. Lethbridge listened, then turned to Fiona with the directness of a
man accustomed to finding the actual engineer in any room of gentlemen.

"And what," he asked, "prevents sedition?"

Fiona thought of every networked system she had ever seen and every committee
that had asked the same question in better furniture.

"Nothing," she said. "Nothing in the wire itself. Only in the institutions
around it."

"Then the institutions," said Lethbridge, "must be arranged correctly."

That afternoon she wrote in her notebook:

`All communications systems become political at scale.`

Then, after a pause:

`The question is not whether they will be used for control. The question is who
controls the control.`

This might have remained an inward irritation had not the postwar years brought
commercial explosion. Shipping firms wanted price and cargo updates. Insurance
houses wanted incident notices. Banks wanted rates. Textile merchants wanted
forecasts of cotton arrivals. The line rooms acquired not only operators but
clerks, messengers, inspectors, and eventually a type of person Fiona knew
instinctively from her own century: the manager who could not build, repair, or
understand the system but felt appointed by Providence to optimize it.

Somewhere in this expansion Fiona herself became, without intending to, a
public name. Not a famous one. That would have been safer or more dangerous, at
least in recognizable forms. She became instead what London made of difficult
women when it could not quite deny them: a person discussed in letters,
mentioned in memoranda, and treated in meetings as though she had just stepped
out and might at any moment step back in to correct the room.

Miss Marsh says the seals will not last.
Miss Marsh insists upon another gauge.
Miss Marsh opposes rapid extension.
Miss Marsh has the support of Faraday in this matter.

Faraday. Even the names around her were changing. Davy climbed. Banks aged.
Younger men became central with shocking speed, as if history were impatient
once it found a usable gradient.

The decisive technical turn of those years was the repeater. Not glamorous. Not
public. But repeaters made range into architecture rather than luck. Messages
could be segmented, restored, forwarded, logged, and counted. With them, a
network ceased to be a line and became a system.

The first repeater office Fiona trusted occupied an upstairs room above a
warehouse whose owner hated the entire enterprise and charged for floor space as
if copper itself were a vice. The apparatus filled the room with an ugly,
steady industry.

- incoming signal
- local detection
- relay restoration
- re-transmission on the next stage
- separate logging of send and receive states

To everyone else it looked like clutter. To Fiona it looked like a future office
building trying to be born through wood, brass, and acid.

She and Faraday spent six days there after an unexplained cascade of message
faults on northbound traffic. On the second day they discovered the technical
cause: one operator, eager and tidy, had polished a contact face with the wrong
abrasive and changed its behavior just enough to turn certainty into statistical
insult.

On the third day Fiona discovered the organizational cause: the operator had not
known he must not do so because no instruction existed, only custom.

"There," she said, holding up the ruined contact as if it were legal evidence.
"That is how systems rot. Through unwritten competence."

The warehouse owner, who had come upstairs merely to complain about fumes, said,
"Madam, I do not see the rot. I see a machine needing a smarter man."

"Then you see exactly the rot," Fiona replied.

The repeater office gave her proof she could carry into every argument after.
Not faster batteries. Not stronger magnets. Systems.

Repeaters implied addressing.
Addressing implied stores.
Stores implied archives.
Archives implied search.
Search implied machines that did more than relay.

The path to green letters on glass still lay far ahead, but she could see the
slope now.

She also saw the shadows it cast.

In `1819`, when unrest in the north sent the government into one of its periodic
convulsions of fear, officials requested temporary monitoring arrangements on
selected traffic routes. Temporary, Fiona noticed, was the preferred adjective
of the permanent state.

She objected in committee. Not successfully.

The committee room was hot despite the season and crowded with the kind of men
who believed confidentiality inhered in mahogany. One of them, a solicitor for a
commercial syndicate, argued that monitoring should be restricted to public
safety matters. Another, representing military interests, argued that public
safety was precisely what states existed to define. Lethbridge, dry as old paper,
listened until the dispute had conveniently clarified the boundary between their
interests, and then proposed review powers broad enough to satisfy all of them.

After the meeting Faraday found her alone in the instrument room, disassembling
a relay she had no need to touch.

"You knew they would do it," he said quietly.

"Knowing and watching are different activities."

"Will you resign?"

She gave him a tired look.

"Into what? Moral purity and no influence?"

He did not answer at once.

"Then what are we to do?"

Fiona set the relay spring down on the cloth.

"Build enough of it that no one office can wholly own it."

Faraday frowned. "Decentralize."

She laughed despite herself. "You make me sound fashionable."

"You are many things, Fiona. Fashionable has never been among them."

It was one of the few times he used her given name. In public she remained Miss
Marsh. In laboratories she had become something harder to classify and therefore
more real.

So they pushed in two directions at once: outward and sideways. More lines, yes.
But also more operators, more local offices, more independent competence outside
Whitehall. Standards published widely enough to become difficult to monopolize.
Instrument practices shared with men in Manchester, Bristol, and Glasgow.
University men drawn into the work before ministries could classify every
procedure.

It was not democracy. Not remotely. But it was distribution, and distribution
was often the nearest historical substitute.

By the early `1820s`, messages could outrun coaches reliably enough that people
began to think in new units. Not days but hours. Not hours but intervals. A
merchant might go to sleep not knowing and wake to an answer from another city.
An admiralty board might delay a decision because fresh information was now a
credible possibility rather than a miracle. Expectation shifted. Time itself, in
small administrative ways, became more crowded.

Fiona saw the change first in language. People no longer said eventually where
they had once said if.

When she realized that, it frightened her more than any minister.
