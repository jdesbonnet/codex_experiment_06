# Chapter 10: The Enchantress of Number

Ada Byron entered the story as a girl with too much attention and, later, as a
woman with the unforgivable talent of understanding structure.

Fiona first saw her at a distance, years before the useful collaboration
began, when Lady Byron brought her daughter to observe a demonstration in which
Babbage was being difficult in front of three men of rank and enjoying himself
immensely. The child watched the mechanism, not the audience. Fiona remembered
that.

When they met properly, Ada was older, sharper, and already accustomed to being
regarded as an ornament by men who thought themselves rational. She had her
mother's discipline and her father's dangerous appetite for scale, which in
combination produced something rarer than brilliance: directed brilliance.

"Mr. Babbage tells me you are the practical conscience of all his better
notions," Ada said on first introduction.

"He says that when he wants me to stay in the room."

"Then I should learn the method."

Ada understood almost at once what many of Babbage's contemporaries did not:
that the engines were not merely calculators. They were processors of sequence.
Manipulators of symbolic procedure. Their brass did not limit their principle.

Fiona had waited years to hear someone say that without being led to it.

"Yes," she said quietly. "That is the point."

They worked first on notation. It sounded modest and therefore harmless, which
made it politically survivable. But notation was power. Fiona had learned it in
medical devices, in regulatory systems, in networking, in every domain where
ambiguity became cost. Ada grasped instinctively that no machinery capable of
general process could flourish without a language that outlived particular
assemblies.

So they built one.

Not alone, and not cleanly. Their notation grew out of workshop marks,
mathematical shorthand, telegraph addressing, and the ugly practical demands of
operators who needed symbols at speed and under stress. Ada gave it elegance.
Fiona gave it failure modes. Between them they produced a style of procedural
description that could pass between engine rooms, telegraph offices, and
mathematical correspondence without fully belonging to any one culture.

Babbage, seeing its utility, immediately claimed to have desired it all along.

Ada rolled her eyes with such precision that Fiona nearly laughed aloud during a
meeting at which two senior men were trying to decide whether women could be
said to contribute to mathematics if they expressed it attractively.

"If they continue in this vein," Ada murmured later, "I shall be tempted to
express a pistol attractively."

The real work began when notation had to survive contact with operators.
Fiona arranged for a test session in a line office rather than a drawing room,
which offended nearly everyone except the people whose judgment mattered.

Two clerks, one mechanic, and an operator with permanent acid stains on his
fingers were given a set of encoded procedures and asked to perform three
things:

- load a recurrent correction pattern into a tabulation process
- alter one routing condition without disturbing the rest
- identify where the notation allowed a dangerous ambiguity

The clerks admired the symmetry. The operator found the first fatal problem in
under ten minutes.

"This mark here and this mark here," he said, tapping two symbols, "will look
the same when copied fast by a tired hand."

Ada did not defend the design. She asked, immediately, "What would you prefer?"

The man hesitated, then offered an uglier sign.

"Use it," Fiona said.

Afterward Ada seemed almost exhilarated.

"He was right," she said.

"Yes."

"And he had no reverence at all for our intention."

"That is why he was useful."

Ada smiled. "You really do worship operators."

"No. I merely know that any language which fails in their hands is a language
for vanity."

The phrase pleased Ada enough that she wrote it down.

Romance remained peripheral in Fiona's life because there was too much else and
because she had, by then, been remade by time into something outside ordinary
domestic expectation. Yet if she loved anyone in that period with uncomplicated
professional affection, it was Ada. Not sentimentally. Not maternally. Simply
with the fierce relief of finding another mind willing to run ahead without
collapsing into fantasy.

Ada's great contribution in this altered century was not merely commentary on
engines. It was the insistence that networks and engines belonged to one future.
She saw, before most, that a message could be more than text, an instruction
more than a command, and a machine more than an instrument of arithmetic.

"The wire is already an abstraction," she told Fiona once, standing over pages
of protocol notes. "It takes a thing said here and makes it a thing written
there. Why should an engine not take a thing written here and make it a process
there?"

Why indeed.

That sentence moved Fiona more than she admitted. It was not new to her, coming
as she did from a century drowned in layered abstractions. But to hear it born
honestly in this one, in Ada's voice, made the whole strange labor feel less
like smuggling and more like cultivation.

Not importation from the future. Growth.

The men around them remained inconsistent. Some patronized. Some conspired.
Some simply extracted. Yet the work advanced. Addressing schemes improved.
Procedure notation stabilized. Network offices began using engine-assisted
tabulation and error checking on high-value traffic. Message stores stopped
being mere ledgers and became structured archives.

One of the most useful arguments Fiona and Ada had was over beauty.

Ada believed beauty in notation mattered because it permitted thought to range.
Fiona believed beauty mattered only if it survived dirt, haste, and boredom.
Neither woman surrendered. The resulting system was better for the conflict.

They developed conventions for:

- named procedures
- parameter substitution
- condition markers
- error returns
- address and store references
- machine-state annotations readable by both mathematicians and operators

Looking at the pages later, Fiona sometimes felt a private vertigo. There it
was, under candlelight, in ink and human argument: the grammar of software,
refusing to call itself software because history had not yet provided the word.

There were evenings in those years when Fiona could stand in a room lit by oil
and still, in some slant of organization or notation, glimpse the old world:
terminals, protocols, queues, message headers, machine-readable intent.

Not yet the visible future.

But its grammar.
