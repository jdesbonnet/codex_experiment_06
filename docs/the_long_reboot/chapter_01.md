# Chapter 1: The Weight of Twenty Kilograms

The first thing Fiona understood was that the suitcase was too heavy.

Not too heavy by airport standards. Not too heavy for a train platform, a
hotel lobby, or the polished optimism of a modern city where every burden
could, sooner or later, be set down on a wheeled surface and forgotten. It was
too heavy for mud.

She stood in it up to the soles of her boots, one hand locked around the
plastic handle of a dark, hard-sided case, the other pressed flat against her
own ribs as if her body might, with enough firm instruction, remember what had
happened.

Nothing did.

There was no tunnel, no white light, no cinematic rupture. One moment she had
been in the garage workshop behind her rented house in Ely, swearing at a bench
power supply and trying to decide whether the regulator failure in a blood-flow
monitor prototype was bad layout, bad luck, or a bad week. The next moment she
was outside in cold air that smelled of wet wool, horse, and distant coal, with
the sky too wide and the silence too physical.

Silence was wrong, actually. There were noises, but they were old noises. Wind
through hedges. Birds. Something wooden knocking somewhere out of sight. A
wheel, perhaps, far off and slow. No road wash. No aircraft. No electric
hum. No low, civilised pressure from a whole planet of powered things doing
their invisible work.

She turned in a full circle.

A lane, rutted and brown. Bare trees. A field enclosed by hedge rather than
wire. No poles. No signs. No asphalt. The light had that hard, thin quality of
winter afternoon, and the clouds sat low enough to feel personal.

The suitcase bit into her palm.

Fiona lowered it carefully and crouched beside it. Her breathing had gone fast
and shallow, which offended her on professional grounds. Panic was inefficient.
It narrowed the field of view and made people stupid. She had spent enough time
in test labs and clinical rooms to recognise the familiar bodily treachery:
adrenaline first, judgment later.

She put both hands on the case and said out loud, very evenly, "Inventory."

The word hung in the air and vanished.

She tried the latches.

Inside, everything was exactly as she had packed it, right down to the notebook
she had intended to leave behind and had, at the last moment, shoved in on the
grounds that any bag with room for two sweaters had room for one more argument
with the future.

Two power banks. Folded solar charger. Ruggedized tablet. E-reader. Printed
notes in clear sleeves. Three bound exercise books in her own handwriting. A
zip pouch of components: LEDs, diodes, MOSFETs, small logic chips, quartz
oscillators, voltage regulators, op-amps, connectors, a few sensors. Stainless
steel calipers. A micrometer. Fine screwdrivers. A loupe. Mechanical pencils.
Pens. Paracetamol. Water purification tablets. A compact multimeter. Spare
cables. Clothing, rolled tight. Underneath everything, laminated pages she had
made for fun on a wet Sunday the previous autumn: machine screws, thread forms,
basic metallurgical heat treatment notes, sulfuric acid process notes, telegraph
circuits, vacuum pump diagrams, galvanic cell chemistry, steam cycle sketches,
Ohm's law, Maxwell too compressed to be decent, and three pages headed, in an
uncharacteristically theatrical hand:

**IF YOU HAD TO RESTART THE WORLD**

At the time, it had seemed like an indulgence. Something between a game and a
private manifesto. A systems-engineering fantasy for the sort of person who
read too much history and distrusted how thin modern life had become.

Now the pages looked less like a joke than a threat.

She sat back on her heels and forced herself to look at the things she had not
packed.

No tent. No firearm. No serious medical kit. No proper boots for long country
walking. No gold. No silver. No universally useful trade goods beyond a handful
of modern components that, to any ordinary person here, would look like cheap
jewellery or broken toys. Most of the real weight was information, and
information, she thought grimly, was only wealth when there was a way to cash
it.

She pulled out the smallest notebook and flipped to the first blank page.

`Cold. Mud lane. Trees bare. Northern hemisphere winter. Britain? Europe?`

She paused.

`No power lines. No modern noise floor. Horse smell. Coal smoke weak, distant.`

Then, beneath that:

`If this is real, survival first. Credibility second. Tools third.`

She stared at the last sentence. It annoyed her because it was true.

Not electricity. Not silicon. Not code. Tools.

There were millions of people in her own century who could explain a
microprocessor and very few who could make a flat surface, a decent bearing, or
a valve that did not leak. She knew enough history to understand the trap. The
world had not waited until the twentieth century because nobody had thought to
invent email. It had waited because iron had to be made consistently, threads
had to match, copper had to be pure enough, glass had to seal, acids had to be
cheap, and people had to trust measurements made by strangers.

The machine before the machine.

The institution before the invention.

She closed the notebook and listened.

Voices.

Not close. Male, by the roughness of them, though distance could lie. A horse
again, definitely this time, harness leather creaking in rhythm. Fiona's whole
body went still.

A more romantic person might have imagined that history arrived with fanfare.
Trumpets. Hoofbeats. A pageant of flags and mud. But this was only a lane, and
she was only a woman in an expensive rain shell kneeling beside a suitcase that
looked as though it had fallen out of the future by administrative error.

She snatched the coat open, stuffed the brighter plastic items down deeper,
dragged a wool jumper over the visible zips, and looked at herself in the black
screen of the tablet.

Wrong.

Hopelessly wrong.

Her hair was tied with a modern elastic band. Her jacket was synthetic in a way
that no eye in 1800 would name but any eye might distrust. The boots were
serviceable, but the stitching and molded sole belonged to factories that did
not yet exist. Even her posture was wrong. She carried herself like somebody
who had spent a life around doors that opened automatically.

The voices came nearer. Fiona shut the case, stood, and tried to lift it with
the composure of a person entirely unsurprised to find herself alone in a
winter lane two centuries from home.

The case barely left the ground.

"Brilliant," she muttered.

There would be a lesson in this later, if there was a later. Twenty kilograms
was a sensible luggage limit in the abstract. In practical terms it was an
anchor. Every kilogram had been chosen by a rational mind solving the wrong
problem. She had packed for leverage and forgotten speed. Packed for rebuilding
the world and forgotten the interval in which one still had to move through it.

The hedge at her right rustled. A robin darted out, absurdly bright, landed on
a branch, and fixed her with the expression of a tiny magistrate.

The voices were clear now. English. That much at least felt like a blessing.
The accent was older than any living accent she knew, rounder in the vowels and
rough at the edges, but intelligible. Men discussing the road. One complaining
about a wheel.

Fiona set the case down again.

If they saw the inside of it, she might gain curiosity and lose safety. If she
lied badly, she might lose both. If she told the truth, and if by some mercy it
was believed, then everything became political at once.

She thought of the pages in the case.

Telegraph relays.
Lead-acid chemistry.
Standard screw threads.
Vacuum pumps.
Magnetic recording.
The first patient, methodical steps toward green letters glowing on glass.

All of it balanced, ridiculous and exact, on the fact that she did not yet have
a dry room, a fire, or anyone obliged to hear her out.

The men came into view around the bend: two labourers walking beside a cart with
one tilted wheel, and a third perched on the shaft, hat low against the cold.
None of them wore uniforms. None looked rich. All three looked strong enough to
be dangerous by accident.

Fiona straightened.

One of them saw her, stopped, and said something she did not catch.

The others looked up.

She knew, with a clean and total certainty, that whatever happened next would
matter more than any equation she had ever memorised.

Not because this was the beginning of a better world.

Because this was the beginning of a world that would now include her.

The man on the cart narrowed his eyes at the suitcase, then at Fiona's face.

"Miss?"

Her own voice, when it came, sounded calmer than she felt.

"Yes," she said. "I wonder if you might help me."

The wind shifted. Coal smoke, stronger now, came down the lane from somewhere
ahead. Beyond the hedge, unseen but suddenly imaginable, there would be houses,
an inn perhaps, a forge, a parish, accounts kept with ink, tools worn smooth by
hands, and a society balanced on habits older than any government that now
existed in her mind.

She looked toward the bend in the road, toward the place the men had come from,
and felt history narrow from theory to distance.

The future, she thought, had always seemed large.

The past was larger.
