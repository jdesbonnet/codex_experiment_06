# Chapter 12: The House of Memory

Storage came by patience rather than revelation.

Punch media had long since become insufficient. Ledgers multiplied. Logs bred
logs. Engine outputs required preservation, sorting, retrieval. The wire had
taught the world to want answers quickly; now the world was drowning in the cost
of remembering what had already been said.

Magnetic recording, when it began to become practical, seemed at first like one
more specialized marvel. Wire. Coated media. Signal traces that could be
written, erased, rewritten. Most men treated it as an extension of telegraphy.
Fiona treated it as civilization's next filing cabinet.

She had been waiting for a form of memory that would no longer require every
useful fact to masquerade as paper. Paper had served. Paper would continue to
serve. But paper made copying expensive, retrieval slow, and procedural reuse a
matter of clerical devotion rather than system design.

The first great storage room they built in London was called, by common usage,
the Archive. Fiona privately called it the House of Memory, because the
mechanical reels, drums, and cabinets within it felt less like office furniture
than organs.

Messages entered.
Messages were sorted.
Indexes were built.
Procedures were stored externally.
Machine states could be reproduced.
Small bundles of encoded instructions, no longer only human text, could be sent,
received, queued, and invoked.

The building chosen for it had once been a warehouse of more ordinary ambition.
Retrofitting it cost money no committee wished to spend and demanded compromises
no engineer should have accepted. Fiona accepted them anyway because historical
progress, she had learned, generally arrived in buildings planned for something
less interesting.

The problems came in layers.

- media degradation
- alignment drift
- write consistency
- retrieval indexing
- argument over ownership of stored procedures
- argument over who might inspect archives and under what authority

The last problem interested government most, which meant it interested Fiona
more than the technical problems, however much she wished otherwise.

Small file transfer, she thought one evening, watching reels turn behind safety
glass. We are doing small file transfer with the furniture of an empire.

Ada saw it too, and said as much in her own way.

"The machine has begun to remember not merely answers," she said, "but habits."

Fiona nodded.

"That is when people start trusting it too much."

Ada smiled faintly. "Only too much?"

The House of Memory changed everything because it made persistence
administrative. Not memory in a human sense. Memory as a managed resource. A
message need not end where it was read. A procedure need not reside where it was
written. A file, though they did not yet use the word with modern casualness,
could become an object independent of paper.

This, more than the display work, made the system feel eerily like home.

Operators now spoke of queues. Of retrieval delays. Of corrupted stores. Of
versioning. Of whether an instruction set copied from Manchester would run
unchanged in London if the engine revisions differed by two mechanical standards
and a relay timing patch.

The arguments were ugly, familiar, and glorious.

One particularly vicious week was consumed by a dispute between a government
calculation office and a private insurer, both of whom claimed authority over a
procedure library used for risk tabulation and coastal shipping forecasts. The
technical question was trivial. The legal question was not. Who owned an
improved procedure once it had been refined in use? The originating office? The
operators who had stabilized it? The ministry that paid for the machine hours?

Fiona listened to three barristers invent a future she recognized and hated.

"They are going to turn methods into property," she said afterward.

Faraday, older and more tired by then but still uncommonly clean in moral line,
answered, "Were they ever anything else?"

"Less explicitly, perhaps."

"That is only another way of saying worse managed."

She hated that he was right.

Fiona had long feared she might die before the world became recognizable to her
again. Now, walking the aisles between cabinets and tape assemblies, she felt
for the first time the opposite fear: that it might become recognizable in the
worst ways as well as the best.

For the House of Memory served ministries as readily as merchants. Police wanted
archives. Finance wanted archives. Naval planning wanted archives. Radicals,
too, when they gained access through friends of friends and quietly borrowed
night time on provincial machines, wanted archives.

One spring morning she found a retrieval clerk asleep at his desk with a stack
of requests fanned around him like accusations. She ought to have been annoyed.
Instead she felt a bleak affection. Progress always claimed it would remove
burden. In practice it redistributed burden to new hands and then called the
arrangement efficiency.

She covered the man with a spare coat, sorted the requests herself, and realized
halfway through that she had become, without any formal decree, one of the old
women of the system: not merely builders of it, but keepers of its temper.

The future had not returned.

It had diversified.
